## Overview

### The Company & Problem Space

The company builds a product sustainability and compliance platform. It creates digital twins of physical products and their supply chains -- connecting bill-of-materials data, procurement information, and environmental datasets to calculate product carbon footprints, run life cycle assessments (LCA), model costs, and generate compliance reports.

The customers are engineering, procurement, and sustainability teams at manufacturing companies who need data-driven decisions about materials, suppliers, and product designs. The core value proposition: upload your product data, and the platform gives you a complete environmental, cost, and compliance picture -- without hiring a team of LCA consultants.

### My Role

I joined as product lead for the AI & Data Science team. The team builds and operates a suite of AI services that sit at the core of the platform's automation layer. Every time a customer uploads product data -- a bill of materials, a list of transactions, component specifications -- our AI services kick in to fill gaps, classify items, map substances to environmental datasets, and ultimately transform raw input into a complete, LCA-ready digital twin.

When I joined, these AI services existed but operated as a loosely connected set of models with limited visibility into performance, no standardized processes for deployment or retraining, and heavy dependency on the AI team for routine operational tasks. My mandate was to professionalize this: make the AI framework reliable, scalable, transparent, and increasingly autonomous.

### The Vision I Set

I authored the team's vision and strategy: **"Transform any product into an LCA-ready digital twin, automatically."**

The north star metric I defined is the percentage of products that can be successfully turned into complete digital twins without manual intervention. This frames everything the team does -- every AI service, every model improvement, every new capability is measured against whether it moves us closer to full automation.

The strategic direction has two axes:

1. **Broaden product compatibility** -- support more industries, more data formats, more product types. The platform started strong in specific verticals; the goal is to make the AI work for any manufacturing product.

2. **Achieve true automation** -- move from "AI-assisted" (human reviews and corrects AI output) to "zero-shot" (AI handles it end-to-end with confidence). This means not just improving accuracy, but building confidence scoring, fallback strategies, and self-assessment capabilities into the AI services.

### The AI Services Landscape

The AI services aren't isolated models -- they work together as a pipeline that turns raw product data into a complete digital twin:

**Data capture** -- a customer uploads raw data (a bill of materials, transaction list, or component spec). The platform ingests and normalizes it.

**Classification** -- a classifier service examines each line item and determines what it is: a chemical compound, an electronic component, a raw material, a generic part. This routes it to the right specialized AI agent.

**Specialized AI agents** -- depending on the product type, the classified item is routed to a domain-specific AI agent. Each agent handles what it knows best: chemical compounds, electronic components, general materials and processes. The agents predict whatever attributes are missing -- synthesis routes, material compositions, environmental categories, processing methods -- to complete the product model for that item.

**Product profile assembly** -- predictions from all agents are combined with known data into a single, complete product model.

**Verification** -- a human-in-the-loop step where domain experts review AI-generated content, accept or correct it, and publish verified models to the shared database.

**Digital twin** -- the final output: a compliance-, cost-, and sustainability-ready product model that can be used for LCA, carbon footprint calculations, and reporting.

A **monitoring and feedback loop** closes the circuit: verification outcomes feed back into the AI services, so every correction improves future predictions. This is the engine behind the vision of "transform any product into a digital twin, automatically."

### What I Changed -- First Year Summary

In my first year, I focused on six areas that collectively transformed how the AI team operates and delivers value:

1. **Strategic AI pivot** -- Identified that the existing classification architecture had hit a ceiling. Led the PoC, presented to the CEO, and redirected the team to an LLM + retrieval-augmented generation approach.
2. **Chemical AI product** -- Shaped and shipped the chemical modeling service from concept to production, opening the chemical sector for the business.
3. **Electronics similarity matching** -- Designed and shipped AI-powered component substitution for electronics BOMs, filling data gaps with transparent, scored alternatives.
4. **Self-service operations** -- Redesigned model training and evaluation so implementation teams operate independently.
5. **Governance & transparency** -- Built the operational foundation: scoreboard, checklists, traceability, SOPs.
6. **Domain mastery** -- Became the SME on the end-to-end data flow from upload to digital twin assembly.

Each of these is described in detail below.

## Project 1: Strategic Pivot from Classification Models to LLM+RAG

### Context & Problem

This was the most consequential technical decision I've driven at the company. It's a strategic pivot of the core AI architecture -- replacing a system that was built before I joined with a fundamentally different approach, based on evidence I gathered and a case I built from scratch.

**What existed when I joined:** The platform's core AI service -- predicting missing product attributes during data import (material type, process, classification) -- was powered by classification models trained on labeled data. These had been developed over two generations by the data science team. They were trained per customer on labeled datasets and predicted discrete categories. The team had deep expertise in this approach and had invested years optimizing it.

**The problems I identified after joining:**

- **Accuracy ceiling.** The second-generation models had been optimized as far as the architecture allowed. Improvements were flattening despite increasing investment in labeled data and feature engineering.
- **Overfitting and brittleness.** Models struggled with novel inputs. If a customer uploaded data with terminology or structures the model hadn't seen in training, predictions degraded. Less common product categories performed poorly.
- **Per-client training burden that didn't scale.** Each customer required their own trained model, and retraining was manual and labor-intensive. Our teams were directly involved in every training cycle. With the customer base growing, this was becoming a bottleneck -- onboarding new clients meant weeks of effort before predictions were usable.
- **No zero-shot capability.** The existing approach couldn't predict anything it hadn't been specifically trained on. Every new customer, every new product category, required labeled data first. This directly conflicted with the company's vision of automating product digital twin creation.

**My hypothesis:** Large language models combined with knowledge base retrieval could fundamentally change this equation. Instead of rigid classification from a fixed training set, the system could retrieve relevant examples from a knowledge base and use an LLM to reason about the best prediction. This would handle novel inputs more gracefully, reduce dependency on per-client training data, and -- critically -- enable zero-shot predictions where the system works on data it has never seen before.

This wasn't an incremental improvement proposal. It was a "we need to change the fundamental technology direction" argument, which meant I had to build the case carefully and present it convincingly.

### My Role & Approach

- **Built the strategic case.** I analyzed model performance trends across customers, mapped feedback patterns from the implementation team, and had detailed conversations with the data science team about the architecture's limitations. I documented everything: where the current approach worked well, where it hit walls, and why those walls were inherent to the architecture rather than solvable with more tuning. I compiled this into a proposal document that laid out the current state, the limitations, and the proposed alternative with a direct comparison.

- **Proposed and led the proof of concept.** Rather than asking leadership to take my word for it, I proposed a structured PoC with clear success criteria. The scope: take the highest-volume prediction task (the core BOM import mapping), build an alternative, and benchmark it head-to-head against the existing model on real customer data.

- **Presented results to the CEO.** The PoC results were compelling. The new approach achieved significantly higher accuracy using a fraction of the training data -- a major improvement over existing models. More importantly, it demonstrated zero-shot capability: meaningful predictions on customer data it had never been trained on. I presented these results to the CEO as the evidence base for why we needed to pivot the AI strategy. The presentation framed it not as "the old system is bad" but as "here's the ceiling we've hit, here's what the new approach can do, and here's why this aligns with where we need to go."

- **Designed the migration architecture.** Once the pivot was approved, I designed how we'd transition without disrupting existing customers. Rather than a risky big-bang replacement, I created an A/B testing infrastructure where both systems could run simultaneously:
  - Per-client switching -- route specific customers to the new system while keeping others on the existing one during transition
  - Automated evaluation pipeline -- comparing predictions from both systems against ground truth data
  - Observability layer -- monitoring latency, cost, accuracy, and failure modes in real time
  - Knowledge base management service -- tooling to populate, update, and version the retrieval knowledge base
  - Mappings and labels management -- ability to configure and adjust how the system maps predictions to platform concepts
  - Rollback capability -- instant switch back to the old system if issues emerged

- **Wrote the full implementation plan.** Four phases across approximately six sprints: foundational service build, production hardening, controlled go-live with selected clients, and feedback loop automation (capturing expert corrections to improve the knowledge base over time).

### Key Decisions & Trade-offs

- **Pursue the pivot despite team attachment to the existing approach.** The data science team had built the AI models and had years of expertise in them. Proposing a replacement required sensitivity. I didn't frame it as "your work is broken" -- I framed it as "your work reached the ceiling this architecture allows, and we need a different architecture to go further." Giving the team ownership of the PoC was critical -- they validated the hypothesis themselves rather than being told what to do.

- **Run both systems in parallel, not replace.** More expensive (double infrastructure during transition) but eliminated risk. We could prove the new approach on real production traffic before committing. This also gave the implementation team and customers confidence -- no one would wake up to a different system overnight.

- **Start with the core use case, not a safe side task.** The team wanted to pilot on a simpler, lower-risk prediction task. I pushed for the real data import mapping because that's where the ceiling was most visible and the business impact was largest. If the new approach couldn't improve the core task, the migration wasn't worth it. Starting with the hard problem was a better use of the PoC.

- **Accept higher per-prediction cost for better accuracy and flexibility.** LLM calls are more expensive than classification model inference. I built cost estimates into the proposal and argued that the reduction in manual training effort and improved customer onboarding speed would more than offset the inference cost increase. I also designed the system with cost controls: caching, batching, and a tiered model approach (smaller/cheaper models for simpler predictions, larger models for complex cases).

- **Invest in evaluation infrastructure before scaling.** Before expanding to more prediction tasks, I prioritized building an automated evaluation framework -- a ground truth testing suite that could objectively compare any two approaches. This slowed initial rollout but gave us confidence in every subsequent decision and became a permanent part of the platform.

### Outcome & Impact

- **Changed the company's AI strategy.** The team's direction shifted from "optimize classification models" to "build a retrieval-augmented prediction platform." This is a fundamentally different and more scalable technical roadmap that aligns with the company's vision of autonomous digital twin creation.
- **The PoC results were decisive.** The accuracy improvement was a clear signal. Zero-shot capability on unseen customer data was the clincher -- it directly addressed the biggest scaling bottleneck.
- **The A/B infrastructure became permanent.** Built for the migration, it's now used for any model comparison or experiment, not just this transition.
- **Reduced onboarding dependency on AI team.** The knowledge retrieval approach uses LLM generalization capabilities along with client-specific context instead of per-client trained models, so new customers can get reasonable predictions without weeks of AI team involvement.
- **Future-proofed the AI stack.** The architecture is model-agnostic -- as better LLMs become available, the system can swap them in without rebuilding the retrieval or integration layers.

### Likely Follow-up Questions

**Q: How did you convince the CEO and leadership to approve a pivot of the core AI system?**
A: With data, not opinions. I structured the PoC specifically to produce the evidence needed: head-to-head comparison on the same customer data, with clear metrics. The difference in accuracy was hard to argue with. But the real convincer was the zero-shot demo -- showing that the new approach could make meaningful predictions on customer data it had never seen. That directly mapped to the business goal of faster onboarding and broader product coverage. I also de-risked the proposal by showing it wouldn't disrupt existing customers (parallel running, per-client switching, rollback capability).

**Q: How did you manage the team through the transition?**
A: By giving them ownership. The data science team ran the PoC, not me. I set the goals and criteria, but they chose the technical approach within the retrieval framework. They saw the results firsthand, which turned potential resistance into enthusiasm. I also made sure to acknowledge that the existing system was well-built -- it had reached its architectural ceiling, not a quality ceiling. That distinction mattered for team morale.

**Q: What was the biggest risk?**
A: Two risks. First, LLM inference cost and latency. Classification models are fast and cheap per call. LLM calls are slower and more expensive. I mitigated this with caching, batching, tiered model selection, and detailed cost projections in the proposal. Second, provider dependency -- using third-party LLM APIs means less control over performance, pricing changes, and outages. I mitigated this by designing the architecture to be provider-agnostic, so we can switch between LLM providers without rebuilding the system.

**Q: How do you evaluate AI model quality?**
A: I built an automated evaluation pipeline that compares predictions against a curated ground truth dataset. Metrics include accuracy (e.g. macro F1 to account for class imbalance), coverage (percentage of inputs that get a prediction), and breakdowns by product category and customer. The key design choice was making this evaluation self-service -- the implementation team can run it without AI team involvement, which also supports the self-service training framework (Project 3).

**Q: Is the migration complete?**
A: The architectural foundation is in place and validated. The rollout follows a controlled pattern: migrate one prediction task at a time, validate with the A/B framework, then expand. The core import mapping was the first and is in production. Additional prediction tasks are being migrated iteratively following the same pattern.

## Project 2: Chemical AI -- Strategic Product That Opened the Chemical Sector

### Context & Problem

This is one of the most strategically important products I've shipped. It didn't just solve a technical problem -- it unlocked an entire business segment.

**The business context:** The platform serves manufacturers who need to understand the environmental impact of their products. For some segments -- the platform had strong coverage. But for the chemical industry, there was a fundamental gap. These companies have bills of materials full of chemical compounds, and to calculate the environmental impact of each chemical, you need a complete model of its synthesis process: what raw materials go in, what reactions occur, what energy is consumed, what emissions are generated, what the yield is.

**The problem before Chemical AI:** When a customer uploaded data and the platform encountered a chemical it didn't have data for, there was no automated fallback. The gap had to be filled manually by the implementation team -- a trained chemist or LCA expert would research the synthesis pathway, look up reaction parameters, calculate mass balances, and assemble everything into a product model. This took hours per chemical and weeks-months per client as a single customer onboarding could involve hundreds of unknown chemicals. It didn't scale. Chemical-sector clients either faced long implementation timelines or incomplete assessments. This was a blocker for growing the business in chemicals, pharma, and specialty materials.

**The opportunity I identified:** Most chemical synthesis modeling follows patterns. If we could automate the synthesis prediction and parameter estimation, we could turn a multi-hour manual process into something that runs in minutes during a regular data import -- no expert involvement needed. This would make the platform self-sufficient for chemical data and remove the dependency on the implementation team for routine chemical modeling.

### My Role & Approach

- **Defined the product vision and strategic positioning.** I framed Chemical AI not just as "another AI service" but as the key capability that enables the company to compete in the chemical sector. The pitch was simple: give the system a chemical, and it returns a complete, LCA-ready product model -- ready to be used in environmental analysis. I positioned this to leadership as a market-expansion play, not just an efficiency improvement.

- **Designed the end-to-end integration.** Chemical AI runs automatically as part of the standard data import. When the platform encounters an unrecognized chemical, it triggers the service, generates a complete product model, and links it back to the original data -- no manual step required. If the predicted synthesis involves input chemicals that are also unknown, the system enriches those recursively up to a configurable depth. Domain experts review AI-generated models before they're published to the shared database for all future imports across all clients. This creates a flywheel: every new customer's data makes the platform smarter for all customers.

- **Drove multiple improvement iterations post-launch.** After the initial release, I led several cycles: like expanding coverage to more chemical types, automating data cleanup steps and improving prediction accuracy based on expert feedback.

### Key Decisions & Trade-offs

- **Scope to "good enough" for launch, then iterate.** The first version covered the most common chemical types and major production markets. We didn't try to handle every edge case before shipping. Some chemicals would fall back to manual processing, but the majority were automated from day one. This was the right call -- waiting for 100% coverage would have delayed the launch by months.

- **Hybrid AI + deterministic design.** Rather than having the AI predict everything, I insisted on splitting the problem. The AI handles uncertainty, research and pre-validation; deterministic logic handles what's calculable (e.g. mass, energy usage etc). This made validation straightforward -- you can verify the chemistry math independently of the AI's predictions -- and made the system trustworthy for domain experts.

- **Multi-market coverage from day one.** I pushed for the service to support multiple production geographies (major global regions) at launch, because customer supply chains are global. The team initially wanted to start with one market. I argued that a single-market service would have too many gaps to be useful for real customer data and would undermine trust in the feature.

- **Verify-then-publish model.** I could have designed it as "auto-apply everything" for speed, or "require expert review for every chemical" for safety. I chose a middle path: auto-apply predictions during import so customers get immediate value, but flag them for expert review before publishing to the shared database. This gives speed for individual clients while maintaining data quality for the platform overall.

### Outcome & Impact

- **Opened the chemical sector for the business.** Before Chemical AI, chemical-sector clients couldn't run LCA at scale on the platform. Now they can onboard and get comprehensive environmental analysis with minimal manual intervention. This is a strategic differentiator -- the company now promotes Chemical AI as a named product capability on its website and in sales conversations.
- **Reduced chemical modeling from hours to minutes.** What previously required a domain expert spending hours per chemical now happens automatically during data import. Implementation teams can focus on edge cases and quality review instead of routine modeling.
- **Removed the scalability bottleneck.** The implementation team was the bottleneck for chemical-sector clients. Now the platform handles routine chemicals autonomously, and the team only intervenes for complex or unusual compounds.
- **Created a data flywheel.** Every verified chemical model gets published to the shared database, improving matching for all future imports across all clients. The platform's chemical coverage grows with every new customer.
- **Multiple improvement iterations shipped:** expanded chemical type coverage, CAS-based identification, automated data cleanup, accuracy improvements from expert feedback loops.

### Likely Follow-up Questions

**Q: How did you validate that the AI predictions were accurate enough to use in real environmental assessments?**
A: We built a validation dataset of chemicals with known creation pathways from published literature and expert review. We compared the AI's results against this ground truth across several dimensions. We set accuracy thresholds per dimension and only launched when all thresholds were met. Post-launch, we added a structured feedback mechanism where domain experts flag incorrect predictions, and those corrections feed back into improvement cycles.

**Q: How did you handle cases where the AI gets it wrong?**
A: Two safety nets. First, all AI-generated models are clearly tagged as predictions, so no one confuses them with verified data. Second, models go through expert review before being published to the shared database. For individual client imports, the predictions are applied immediately (so the client gets value fast) but with clear visibility that it's AI-generated and can be overridden. The manual modeling path always remains available as a fallback.

**Q: How did you prioritize which chemicals to support first?**
A: By analyzing customer data. We looked at which chemicals appeared most frequently in customer uploads and were currently being modeled manually. The top-volume chemicals became the priority for the first version. This ensured maximum impact from day one. Edge cases and rare chemicals are handled in subsequent iterations or fall back to manual processing.

**Q: You say this "opened the chemical sector" -- how do you quantify that?**
A: Before Chemical AI, chemical-sector deals had long implementation timelines because of the manual modeling bottleneck. The platform could technically handle the analysis, but getting the data in was the problem. After Chemical AI, the onboarding time for chemical-heavy clients dropped significantly because the bulk of chemical modeling happens automatically. Sales can now pitch the platform to chemical and pharmaceutical companies with confidence that the data gap problem is solved. The company even created a dedicated product page for this capability -- it's a named capability in the go-to-market materials.

**Q: What was the hardest part of this project?**
A: Getting the integration right. The AI prediction itself was one piece, but making it work seamlessly inside the existing data import pipeline -- with recursive enrichment, proper error handling, correct format mapping, and clear traceability -- was where most of the complexity lived. I spent significant time defining the exact data mapping between the AI service output and the platform's native format, because any mismatch would break downstream analyses. The second hardest part was managing the balance between automation and expert trust -- moving too fast would erode confidence in the AI, moving too slow would undermine the business case.

## Project 3: Self-Service AI Model Performance Framework

### Context & Problem

Every customer's AI model needs periodic retraining as their product data evolves. Before I joined, this was a heavily manual process:

1. The implementation team would collect new training data from the customer.
2. They'd send it to the AI team for data transformation and formatting.
3. The AI team would run the training pipeline, generate results, and analyze performance.
4. Back-and-forth iterations between the AI team and the implementation team to assess whether the retrained model was good enough.
5. The AI team would deploy the updated model.

This created two problems:
- **Bottleneck.** The AI team became a bottleneck for routine operations. Every retraining request consumed AI engineering time that should have been spent on product improvement.
- **Opacity.** The implementation team and customers had no way to independently assess model quality. They depended entirely on the AI team's judgment about whether a model was "good enough."

### My Role & Approach

- **Redesigned the training approach.** The existing training output was hard to interpret -- useful for data scientists, meaningless for implementation consultants or customers. I redesigned it as a structured performance report with clear metrics: accuracy, coverage rates, comparison to previous model version, and specific examples of what improved and what degraded.

- **Built it as self-service.** I defined requirements for a self-service training tool where the implementation team can upload new training data, trigger a retraining run, and receive the redesigned performance report -- all without involving the AI team. The tool guides them through data format requirements and flags common issues.

- **Defined quality thresholds.** I established clear, documented thresholds for what constitutes acceptable model performance. This gave the implementation team a decision framework: if accuracy is above X% and coverage is above Y%, the model can be deployed. If not, escalate to the AI team. This removed subjective judgment from routine cases.

- **Built per-client traceability.** Each retraining run is logged with the training data used, the resulting metrics, and who approved the deployment. This creates an audit trail -- important for customer trust and for debugging when predictions seem wrong.

### Key Decisions & Trade-offs

- **Optimize for implementation team autonomy, not AI team convenience.** The easiest fix would have been to make the AI team's process faster. Instead, I redesigned the workflow to remove the AI team from the loop entirely for routine cases. This was harder upfront but fundamentally more scalable.

- **Structured report over dashboard.** I considered building a real-time dashboard but chose a structured report format instead. The implementation team works in discrete cycles (retrain, evaluate, deploy), not continuous monitoring. A report they can share with customers and reference in meetings was more useful than a live dashboard.

- **Clear escalation criteria.** Rather than making the tool handle every scenario, I defined explicit criteria for when to escalate to the AI team. This kept the self-service scope manageable while ensuring complex cases still got expert attention.

### Outcome & Impact

- The AI team was removed from the critical path for routine model retraining. Implementation teams can now handle the full cycle independently.
- Retraining turnaround time dropped significantly -- from days (waiting for AI team availability) to hours (self-service execution).
- Customers gained visibility into their model's performance through shareable reports, increasing trust in the AI services.
- The AI team reclaimed time previously spent on operational tasks, redirecting it to product development and research.

### Likely Follow-up Questions

**Q: How did the implementation team react to taking on more responsibility?**
A: Mixed initially. Some welcomed the autonomy; others were nervous about making quality decisions they previously delegated. The key was the clear thresholds and the structured report -- it gave them confidence that they weren't making judgment calls in the dark. I also ran training sessions and created documentation.

**Q: How do you prevent quality degradation when non-experts manage model training?**
A: Three safeguards: (1) automated quality thresholds that block deployment if metrics are below minimum standards, (2) comparison to the previous model version -- you can't deploy something worse, (3) clear escalation path when the tool flags anomalies. The system is designed to be safe by default.

## Project 4: Operational Foundation for AI Services

### Context & Problem

When I joined, the AI services were technically functional but operationally immature:

- **No centralized performance visibility.** There was no single view of how models were performing across all customers. You had to dig into individual training logs to understand accuracy.
- **No standardized deployment process.** Deploying a new model version involved informal steps that varied by team member. There was no checklist, no sign-off process, no rollback plan.
- **No per-client traceability.** It was difficult to answer basic questions like "which model version is this customer running?" or "when was their model last retrained?"
- **Minimal documentation.** Processes and product documentation lived in people's heads, not in written SOPs. This made onboarding slow and created key-person risk.
- **No transparency for stakeholders.** Neither the implementation team nor customers could see how the AI was performing without asking the AI team directly.

This is the classic "AI works in the lab but isn't production-grade" problem. The models were good, but the operational wrapper was missing.

### My Role & Approach

- **AI Performance Scoreboard.** I designed and implemented a centralized dashboard that tracks model accuracy, coverage, and key metrics across all customer accounts. This gives the team, management, and implementation consultants a single source of truth for AI quality.

- **Deployment checklists.** I led the team to create standardized deployment checklists for every AI service: pre-deployment validation steps, testing requirements, sign-off process, rollback instructions. These replaced informal "just push it" practices with a repeatable, auditable process.

- **Per-client model registry.** I established clear tracking of which model version each customer is running, when it was last trained, what training data was used, and what performance metrics it achieved. This answers the "which model is running where?" question instantly.

- **Documentation & SOPs.** I wrote comprehensive documentation for every AI service: what it does, how it's trained, what its limitations are, and how to troubleshoot common issues. I also created standard operating procedures for recurring tasks (retraining, deploying, responding to quality issues).

- **Incident response process.** I defined how the team responds when an AI prediction causes a customer-visible problem: triage, root cause analysis, communication template, and corrective action tracking.

### Key Decisions & Trade-offs

- **Documentation-first culture.** I made documentation a requirement for every change, not an afterthought. This slowed the team down initially but paid off quickly when people needed to troubleshoot issues or onboard new team members.

- **Scoreboard as a forcing function.** Making performance visible to stakeholders outside the AI team created accountability. The team initially resisted this transparency -- it's uncomfortable to have your model's weaknesses visible to everyone. But it drove improvement because poor performance was now visible, not hidden in logs.

- **Pragmatic scope.** I didn't try to build a perfect MLOps platform. I focused on the minimum viable governance that would make the AI services trustworthy and manageable. Checklists over automation. Documentation over tooling. Transparency over perfection.

### Outcome & Impact

- Stakeholders outside the AI team can now independently assess AI performance without asking the data scientists.
- Deployment failures decreased because the checklist catches issues before they reach production.
- Onboarding time for new team members decreased significantly -- they can read the documentation instead of relying on tribal knowledge.
- Customer trust improved because the implementation team can show performance data during customer reviews.
- The operational foundation became the basis for the self-service framework (my other project) -- you can't give others self-service access without first having clear processes and documentation.

### Likely Follow-up Questions

**Q: This sounds like basic engineering hygiene. Why wasn't it already in place?**
A: Common pattern in AI teams. The team was research-oriented -- brilliant at building models, less focused on operational maturity. When the team was small and the customer base was limited, informal processes worked. As the platform scaled, those processes broke. My contribution was recognizing the gap, prioritizing it alongside feature work (which always feels more urgent), and actually doing the work of writing documentation and building processes.

**Q: How did you balance operational work with feature development?**
A: I didn't treat them as separate. Every new feature had to come with documentation and a deployment checklist. Every model improvement had to include updated performance metrics in the scoreboard. By embedding operational work into the feature delivery process, it stopped being "extra work" and became part of the definition of done.

## Project 5: Domain Expertise -- Data Import Pipeline & AI Service Architecture

### Context & Problem

The AI services don't exist in isolation. They're part of a larger data pipeline: a customer uploads raw data (a bill of materials, a transaction list, a component specification), the platform transforms and validates it, the AI services make predictions to fill gaps, and the result is assembled into a complete product model used for sustainability analysis.

When I joined, no single person understood the entire pipeline end-to-end. The data engineering team owned the import and transformation layer. The AI team owned the prediction services. The platform team owned the model assembly and analysis layer. Each team understood their piece but not how the pieces connected -- and certainly not how a problem in one layer propagated to another.

This created recurring issues:
- Data quality problems in the import layer would cause AI prediction failures, but the root cause wasn't identified quickly because the AI team didn't understand the import logic.
- AI predictions that were technically correct in isolation would produce nonsensical results after model assembly, because the AI team didn't understand downstream constraints.
- Feature requests from customers were often misrouted between teams because nobody could trace the full data journey.

### My Role & Approach

- **Mapped the full pipeline.** I traced the data flow from customer upload through every transformation, API call, AI prediction, and assembly step. I documented which service is responsible for what, where data is transformed, where predictions are inserted, and what validation happens at each stage. This had never been done comprehensively before.

- **Became the translation layer.** I positioned myself as the person who speaks all three languages: data engineering, AI/ML, and platform/domain. When the implementation team reports a problem, I can trace it to the right root cause and the right team. When the AI team proposes a change, I can assess its downstream impact.

- **Used domain knowledge for product decisions.** Understanding the full pipeline is what allowed me to make good decisions on the other projects. For example, the Chemical AI service requirements were informed by knowing exactly how the platform assembles chemical product models. The LLM+RAG architecture was designed with knowledge of how the prediction service is called within the import pipeline.

### Key Decisions & Trade-offs

- **Invest time in understanding before proposing changes.** I spent significant time in the first months just learning -- reading code, tracing data flows, sitting with implementation consultants watching imports happen. This delayed my ability to drive changes but made every subsequent decision better-informed.

- **Document for others, not just myself.** I turned my understanding into documentation that the whole team can reference. This reduced key-person risk (including my own) and helped new team members ramp up faster.

- **Bridge-building over territory.** Rather than claiming ownership of cross-team issues, I worked as a facilitator -- helping each team understand how their work affects others. This was more effective than organizational restructuring.

### Outcome & Impact

- Cross-team debugging time decreased because there's now documentation of the full pipeline and a person who can quickly trace issues to root causes.
- AI service requirements are better-specified because they account for upstream data quality realities and downstream model assembly constraints.
- The implementation team has a go-to person who can explain complex AI behavior in business terms -- reducing miscommunication with customers.
- Pipeline documentation became a shared artifact that all teams reference, improving alignment on how the system actually works versus how each team assumes it works.

### Likely Follow-up Questions

**Q: How do you stay current as the system evolves?**

## Project 6: Closest Match -- AI-Powered Component Substitution for Electronics

### Context & Problem

In electronics manufacturing, bills of materials contain manufacturer part numbers (MPNs) that reference specific components -- capacitors, resistors, ICs, and so on. To calculate the environmental impact of each component, the platform needs detailed material composition data (typically from industry-standard declaration files like IPC documents).

The problem: not every MPN in a customer's BOM has composition data available in the platform's database. When data is missing for a component, that line item becomes a gap in the sustainability analysis -- either it's left incomplete or an analyst has to manually research and find a substitute. At scale, with BOMs containing hundreds or thousands of line items, these gaps add up fast and undermine the completeness of the assessment.

The opportunity I saw: electronics components that are technically similar (same category, similar specifications) tend to have comparable environmental and material profiles. If we could automatically find the most similar component that we do have data for, we could fill those gaps without manual effort -- turning an incomplete assessment into a comprehensive one.

### My Role & Approach

- **Defined the product concept.** I framed this as a "smart fallback" within the existing data import pipeline. When the standard matching process (exact MPN match, supplier lookup, part number search) fails to find a component with impact data, the Closest Match service kicks in as an automated last resort. This positioning was important -- it's not replacing existing matching, it's extending it.

- **Designed the end-to-end flow along with the matching algorithm.** I defined an approach for how the gap could be automatically filled with the right level of transparency so that experts and clients can review and confirm or update provided results.

- **Managed integration and traceability.** Every AI-generated match is tagged so it's visible in quality reports. The implementation team and customers can see exactly which BOM lines used AI substitution versus exact matching, review the alternatives, and override if needed.

### Key Decisions & Trade-offs

- **Fallback, not replacement.** I deliberately positioned Closest Match as the last step in the matching hierarchy, not a parallel option. Exact matches are always preferred. The AI only activates when standard methods fail. This kept the feature low-risk -- it fills gaps without changing the behavior for items that already match well.

- **Equal weights as the starting point.** For the similarity scoring, the initial version assigns equal weight to all technical properties within a component category. This is intentionally simple. Tuning weights per category is a planned iteration, but launching with equal weights let us ship faster and collect real usage data to inform future tuning.

- **Transparency over black-box.** I insisted on making the match type visible in quality reports and providing multiple alternatives (not just the top one). Some stakeholders wanted to auto-apply the best match silently. I argued that for a new AI feature, user trust comes from visibility -- customers need to see what the AI did and have the option to override it.

- **Private data as an extension, not a separate feature.** Rather than building a separate matching flow for private data, I designed it as an expansion of the existing pool. This meant the same ranking logic, same similarity scoring, same UI -- just a larger set of candidates. This kept the implementation simple and the user experience consistent.

### Outcome & Impact

- Data with previously unmatched components now get automated suggestions, improving the completeness of sustainability assessments significantly.
- Reduced manual effort for implementation consultants who previously had to research substitutes for missing components by hand.
- The feature introduced a reusable pattern: the concept of "AI-powered fallback with transparency" is now being considered for other parts of the platform where exact matching fails.

### Likely Follow-up Questions

**Q: How do you know the AI substitution is accurate enough for environmental analysis?**
A: The core assumption is validated by domain expertise: technically similar electronic components have comparable environmental profiles. Using that we built a quantitative measure of how close the suggestion is. We also expose all alternatives so experts can review and override. The feature is a pragmatic tradeoff -- a reasonable proxy is better than a blank gap in the analysis, and the transparency makes it easy to challenge or correct.

**Q: How do you handle cases where no similar component exists in the database?**
A: If the similarity scores for all candidates fall below a minimum threshold, the system doesn't suggest a match -- the line item stays unmatched. We don't force a bad suggestion. The threshold is configurable per deployment, so implementation teams can adjust sensitivity based on client expectations.

**Q: How did you decide on the scoring algorithm?**
A: I designed it to be simple, explainable, and extensible. Weighted average of per-attribute similarity scores is something a non-technical stakeholder can understand ("it matched on 7 out of 10 specs, weighted by importance"). More sophisticated approaches (embeddings, learned similarity) are possible future iterations, but the current approach builds trust because users can inspect and understand every score.

**Q: What's the relationship between this and the LLM+RAG migration?**
A: They're complementary but independent. Closest Match solves a specific problem (missing component data in electronics BOMs) with a similarity-based approach. The LLM+RAG migration addresses the broader prediction task (filling missing attributes across all product types). Closest Match could eventually be enhanced with LLM-based reasoning, but it works well as a standalone feature with its current algorithm.

## Ongoing: AI Enrichment Services Portfolio

Beyond the major projects above, I defined and shipped several additional AI enrichment services that extend the platform's automation coverage. Each follows the same pipeline pattern: trigger during data import when a value is missing, predict it with AI, record provenance, let experts review and override.

**Product classification enrichment.** Products imported into the platform often lack standardized category information, which makes comparison and validation difficult. I wrote the PRD for an AI service that automatically assigns categories from an industry-standard hierarchy of 48,000+ classes. The key design challenges were handling the scale of the taxonomy (too large for a single LLM context window -- I evaluated hierarchical vs. retrieval-based approaches and recommended the latter), defining the priority logic (file value beats existing value beats AI prediction), and setting confidence thresholds where leaving a field blank is better than assigning a wrong category. The service enriches products across BOM, process, and transaction imports.

**Spend sector prediction for Scope 3 emissions.** Companies tracking Scope 3 emissions need every business transaction mapped to an environmental classification. Doing this manually doesn't scale. I defined an AI service that predicts the environmental spend sector based on transaction attributes (item name, material group, supplier, spend category). The model is configurable per client -- it can operate against the full sector hierarchy or a client-specific subset to focus predictions on relevant classifications. Like other services, it integrates into the standard transaction import flow and records AI provenance metadata (service version, prediction date, confidence) for traceability.

These aren't individually transformative stories -- they're the steady work of extending the platform's automation coverage, one data gap at a time. Each required a PRD, integration design, enrichment logic, confidence handling, and coordination with engineering and data science. Together with the major projects, they represent the breadth of the AI service portfolio I manage.
