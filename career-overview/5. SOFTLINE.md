# Part 5: Softline / Early Career (Aug 2004 -- Nov 2010)

## Overview

Softline is where the career started. Six years as a software analyst and engineer, working across supply chain management, electronic document management, and database administration. This was pure technical work -- no "product manager" title, no roadmap ownership -- but it shaped the foundation everything else was built on.

## What I Did

I joined as a Junior Software Analyst straight out of university and grew into a Senior Software Analyst over six years. The work covered:

- **Supply chain management systems** -- designed and implemented SCM modules. This meant understanding logistics workflows end-to-end: procurement, inventory, fulfillment. The domain forced me to think in systems -- how data flows across organizational boundaries, where processes break down, where automation helps and where it creates new problems

- **Electronic document management** -- built document management solutions for enterprise clients. Workflow automation, version control, access management, audit trails. The work was detail-oriented and process-heavy -- good training for later requirements engineering

- **Database administration** -- managed production databases. Performance tuning, schema design, backup and recovery planning. Hands-on work with data at scale, which gave me an intuitive understanding of data modeling that most PMs don't have

## Why This Period Matters

Three things from the Softline years carried forward into every role that followed:

1. **Systems thinking from the inside out.** Building SCM systems taught me how enterprise software actually works -- not the slide deck version, but the messy reality of data integrity, integration failures, and edge cases. When I later managed products, I could have technical conversations that engineers took seriously because I'd been on the other side.

2. **Requirements engineering instinct.** Document management projects were specification-heavy. Clients couldn't articulate what they wanted; I had to extract it through questions, prototypes, and iteration. This became the core PM skill I used at Luxoft, EPAM, and Makersite -- the ability to turn vague business needs into buildable specifications.

3. **Data as a first-class concern.** Database work gave me a respect for data quality, schema design, and the cost of getting data models wrong. At Luxoft, this directly applied to financial data platforms. At Makersite, it applies to AI training data and knowledge base design. Most PMs treat data as someone else's problem; I treat it as the foundation everything sits on.

## The Transition

By 2010, I'd outgrown the analyst role. The technical work was satisfying, but I kept finding myself in the gap between what clients needed and what engineering teams were building. I spent more time clarifying requirements, negotiating scope, and aligning stakeholders than writing code. Moving to Luxoft as a Product Owner formalized what was already happening: I was better at deciding *what* to build than building it myself.

---

*This section is deliberately brief. In interviews, the Softline period is relevant for "tell me about your background" and "how did you get into product management" -- not as a standalone deep-dive. The key message: strong technical foundation, systems thinking, and a natural drift toward the product side.*
