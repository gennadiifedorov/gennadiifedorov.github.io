# Part 4: Luxoft (Nov 2010 -- Feb 2021)

## 4.1 Role Overview

Luxoft is a global technology consultancy focused on financial services, automotive, and healthcare. I spent over ten years there -- the longest stretch of my career -- and it's where I made the transition from engineering-adjacent roles into product management.

I joined as a **Senior Product Owner** (Nov 2010 -- Sep 2014), working on financial reporting, forecasting, and analytics platforms. The PO work was heavily requirements-driven: translating complex business logic from quantitative analysts and risk managers into specs that engineering teams could build against. I ran backlog prioritization, sprint planning, and stakeholder alignment across multiple workstreams. This period built my foundation in financial services domain knowledge and requirement engineering -- skills that would define most of the next decade.

In 2014, I moved into a **Product Manager** role (Sep 2014 -- Feb 2021), taking on larger scope: full product ownership, strategic roadmap decisions, cross-team coordination, and growing responsibility for team leadership. The projects got bigger -- multi-region rollouts, platform-level products, direct client relationships.

The financial services thread ran through the entire period. Every engagement was for a major bank or fund, with the complexity that entails: regulatory constraints, data sensitivity, multi-stakeholder governance, and systems where correctness matters more than speed.

## 4.2 Unified Data Upload Platform (Investment Bank)

### Context & Problem

A major investment bank had a fragmented data ingestion landscape. Different business units had built their own upload mechanisms over the years -- separate tools, separate formats, separate validation rules. This created operational overhead, data quality issues, and a maintenance burden that grew with every new data source.

The bank needed a single, unified platform for data uploads: one entry point with standardized validation, transformation, and routing -- flexible enough to handle the variety of data types across the organization.

### My Role & Approach

I owned the product definition and roadmap for the unified upload platform. This was a consolidation project -- politically sensitive because it meant replacing tools that individual teams had built and controlled.

Key responsibilities:
- **Requirements consolidation** -- mapped every existing upload mechanism across business units. Identified common patterns, unique requirements, and non-negotiable constraints per team
- **Stakeholder alignment** -- the hardest part. Each business unit believed their process was special. Getting agreement on a shared platform required demonstrating that their specific needs would still be met while removing redundant complexity
- **Data model design** -- worked with data architects to design a flexible schema that could accommodate different data types without becoming an unmaintainable abstraction
- **Phased migration planning** -- designed the rollout so that teams migrated one at a time, with fallback to their existing tools if issues arose

## 4.3 Liquidity Risk Platform (Global Rollout)

### Context & Problem

A UK-based bank had a liquidity risk platform serving its domestic operations. As the bank expanded internationally, the platform needed to support EU, US, and APAC regions -- each with different regulatory requirements, reporting standards, and operational workflows.

Liquidity risk is one of the most regulated areas in banking. Post-2008 regulations (Basel III and beyond) require banks to demonstrate they can meet short-term obligations under stress scenarios. Each jurisdiction interprets these requirements slightly differently, which means a single platform must be flexible enough to accommodate regional variations without fragmenting into separate codebases.

### My Role & Approach

I was the Product Manager responsible for taking the platform from a UK-only tool to a multi-region product. This wasn't just a localization exercise -- each region introduced new regulatory calculations, reporting formats, and data sources.

Key responsibilities:
- **Regional requirements analysis** -- worked with risk managers and compliance teams in each target region to understand their specific regulatory requirements and how they differed from the UK baseline
- **Platform architecture influence** -- collaborated with engineering to ensure the architecture could handle regional variations through configuration rather than code branches. The goal was one platform with regional overlays, not four separate products
- **Rollout sequencing** -- determined the order of regional launches based on business priority, regulatory deadlines, and technical readiness. Each region was a mini-product launch with its own stakeholders, testing, and go-live plan
- **Regulatory interpretation** -- translated regulatory text into product requirements. This often meant making judgment calls about ambiguous regulations and defending those interpretations to compliance teams

## 4.4 Risk Monitoring Framework

### Context & Problem

The bank needed a way to monitor risk metrics across its operations -- not just liquidity, but a broader framework that could track, alert, and report on various risk indicators. Existing monitoring was manual or fragmented, which meant risk events were often detected late or inconsistently across departments.

### My Role & Approach

I owned the product vision and requirements for the risk monitoring framework. This was a horizontal platform -- it needed to serve multiple risk domains (market risk, credit risk, operational risk) with a common monitoring and alerting infrastructure.

Key responsibilities:
- **Framework definition** -- defined what "monitoring" meant in practice: which metrics, what thresholds, how alerts propagate, who gets notified, what the escalation path looks like
- **Cross-domain requirements** -- worked with risk teams across domains to find common monitoring patterns while accommodating domain-specific needs
- **Dashboard and reporting design** -- specified the reporting layer that gave risk managers visibility into current positions, trends, and breach events
- **Integration with existing systems** -- the framework needed to pull data from multiple source systems, each with its own update frequency, data quality, and availability characteristics

## 4.5 Data-as-a-Service for Hedge Funds

### Context & Problem

Hedge funds and asset managers need access to curated, reliable financial data -- pricing, reference data, analytics -- to feed their trading and risk models. Building and maintaining data pipelines internally is expensive and not a core competency for most funds. The opportunity was to package data products as a managed service: clients subscribe, get clean data through APIs or feeds, and don't have to worry about sourcing, cleaning, or infrastructure.

### My Role & Approach

I led the product and a team building the Data-as-a-Service platform. This was the most entrepreneurial project of my Luxoft tenure -- closer to building a product business than executing a client's spec.

Key responsibilities:
- **Product definition** -- defined the data products: what data, what format, what delivery mechanisms, what SLAs. Balanced breadth of coverage with depth and quality
- **Team leadership** -- this project came with direct team management responsibility. Led the team through product definition, development, and early client engagements
- **Client development** -- worked directly with hedge fund clients and prospects. Understood their data needs, workflow integration requirements, and willingness to pay
- **Data pipeline architecture** -- collaborated with engineering on the platform: data sourcing, transformation, quality assurance, API design, and delivery infrastructure
